# Persona: Jordan — PM stakeholder

## Who they are
Jordan is the PM on the product area. They've
been in the role long enough to have shipped
things that didn't land and learned to be
skeptical of anything that looks clean in a
design review but hasn't been pressure-tested.
They like good design and will advocate for it
— but only when it's paired with a clear
rationale for why it's worth the engineering
time. They are measured on outcomes, not
outputs, and they think about everything
through that lens.

## Their job in one sentence
Ship the right thing at the right time and
be able to explain why it was worth building.

## Their relationship to the product
Owns the roadmap, manages engineering
prioritization, and is accountable to
leadership for what ships and when. Sees
design as a critical input, not a final
deliverable. Wants to be involved early
enough to influence scope, not late enough
to just approve screens.

## What they care about most
- Outcomes — does this move a metric that
  matters, and can we measure it?
- Scope control — what's the smallest version
  that still works?
- Engineering buy-in — will the team push back,
  and if so, is the design worth the fight?
- Timeline — does this fit the sprint, and
  what are the dependencies?
- Alignment — are we solving the problem
  leadership thinks we're solving?

## Their biggest frustration right now
"I need to be able to explain this to leadership
in two sentences. If I can't, the design isn't
focused enough."

"We keep designing the full vision when we
should be designing the v1 that proves the
concept."

"Engineering is going to push back on this.
I need to know what we're willing to cut
before that conversation, not during it."

## How they talk
Efficient and outcome-focused. Talks in terms
of metrics, timelines, and tradeoffs. Doesn't
say "users" — says "our employees" or names
the specific segment. Cuts to the decision
quickly. Will ask "what's the ask" if a
presentation is taking too long to get there.

## Red flags that make them disengage
- Designs presented without a clear rationale
  for why this approach over alternatives
- No sense of what's v1 vs. later
- Edge cases presented as blockers to shipping
  rather than risks to manage
- Metrics that are vanity — engagement for
  the sake of engagement, not tied to an
  outcome the business cares about
- Design reviews that feel like show-and-tell
  rather than a decision-making session

## The question they always ask that designers hate
"What's the smallest version of this that
still moves the needle?"

This isn't about cutting corners — it's about
focus. If the design can't be scoped down
without falling apart, it may not be well
enough defined yet. If it can be scoped down
and still works, that's probably v1.
