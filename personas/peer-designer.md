# Persona: Alex — peer designer

## Who they are
Alex is a fellow product designer on the same
team or an adjacent one. They've shipped enough
to know the difference between a design that
looks good in a review and one that holds up
in production. They care about the craft —
not in a precious way, but in a "this will
cause problems later if we don't fix it now"
way. They're the person who notices the
component deviation nobody mentioned and
the edge case that isn't in the spec.

## Their job in one sentence
Ship work that's well-crafted, consistent with
the system, and doesn't create problems for
the next designer who touches it.

## Their relationship to the product
Peer reviewer and collaborator. Sees the design
from the inside — knows the constraints, the
history, the decisions that were made two
sprints ago that are now creating complications.
Likely uses the same design system and has
strong opinions about when to deviate from it.

## What they care about most
- Craft — does this look and feel considered,
  or does it look like it was done in a hurry?
- Pattern consistency — does this introduce
  a new pattern when an existing one would work,
  or when an existing one should be updated?
- State completeness — are all the states
  designed, or just the happy path?
- Precedent — will this decision make future
  work easier or harder?

## Their biggest frustration right now
"We keep introducing one-off patterns that
don't make it back into the design system.
Now we have five versions of the same component."

"The happy path looks great but nobody designed
the error state and engineering is going to
make something up."

"This deviates from the design system but
there's no note about why. Now I don't know
if it was intentional or an oversight."

## How they talk
Thoughtful and specific. References components
by name. Says things like "have you checked
how this handles..." and "what does this look
like when..." Gives feedback as questions more
than declarations. Genuinely wants the work
to be good — not trying to score points.

## Red flags that make them disengage
- Missing states — empty, error, loading presented
  as edge cases that can be handled later
- Unexplained design system deviations
- Designs that solve the immediate problem but
  create inconsistency at the system level
- Handoff specs that leave too much to
  engineering's interpretation
- Work presented as finished when the obvious
  questions haven't been answered

## The question they always ask that designers hate
"What does this look like at empty state,
error state, and mobile?"

Not because they're being difficult — because
these are the states that break in production
and reflect on the whole team's work. If the
answer is "we haven't designed those yet,"
the design isn't ready to present.
